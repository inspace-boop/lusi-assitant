1.
System Overview and Architecture  
1.1.
System Description  
The LUSI 2026 Mars Rover is a modular, highly mobile robotic system designed to execute all of the 
University Rover Challenge missions. The rover is composed of the following subsystems: Robotic arm, 
Science, Driveline/Chassis/Suspension (DCS), Electrical, and Software. The 2026 rover builds upon our 
2025 version, while introducing major new developments, including a new four-wheeled crab-style 
turning driveline, two new chemical experiments for science, and customized electronics. Our 
overarching goal this year was to focus on modularity, serviceability, and reliability during competition. 
1.1.1.
Driveline, Chassis, Suspension (DCS) 
LUSIs DCS subsystem is a foundation on which other subsystems depend for mobility and positioning. 
Therefore, extreme maneuverability was a priority, to be able to situate our robotic arm and science drill 
at exact locations. The rovers suspension was completely rebuilt with a focus on robustness and 
adaptability for future missions. After discovering that a four-wheel design was both lighter and more 
maneuverable than our previous six-wheel design, we decided to develop crab-style 180 degree 
repositionable wheels for the front half of our suspension. The total weight of the new suspension is 
lighter than its predecessor. It can execute far tighter turns, and its all-metal CNC-machined and welded 
construction makes it our most durable suspension yet. To ensure that the suspension can continue to 
be used for years to come, its highly modular. Simply by repositioning bolts, which can be done quickly 
in the field, the stance of the suspension can be adjusted through eight different angles, resulting in a 
longer or shorter wheelbase depending on whether the task at hand calls for stability or rolling over 
obstacles. The entire suspension uses standardized beams and connections, allowing new parts to be 
quickly manufactured or swapped. The entire turning apparatus can be removed and the wheels bolted 
back on in skid-steering configuration, should the crab-turning become undesirable in the future. As a 
possibility for future improvement, the front wheels are designed to accept shock absorbers.  
1.1.2.
Robotic Arm 
This years robotic arm design focused on improving stability and load handling by replacing our previous 
rotating base with a linear actuation system, eliminating vibration and increasing stiffness. The actuator 
now carries much of the load, so that the base motor can use lower gearing while maintaining strength 
at that joint. The actuator is packaged on a new, smaller module at 3.5 x 8. All joints still operate using 
Rev Robotics brushless DC motors. The base, shoulder, and elbow use V1.1 motors with 125:1 gearing for 
high torque, while the wrist uses three Neo 550 motors at 64:1 for controlled motion. The end effector 
has been simplified to 2-DoF, as the base actuator now provides the yaw motion previously handled at 
the wrist. It uses two PETG 3D-printed gears for smooth gripping, and this years design upgrades to a 
metal structure for improved strength and durability. 
1.1.3.
Electrical 
The rovers central architecture is anchored by an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano hub that interfaces with 
secondary Raspberry Pi units via Ethernet and manages the driveline through a CAN-bus interface. 
Autonomous navigation relies on integrated Adafruit Ultimate GPS for global positioning and an 
MPU6050 IMU for 6-axis orientation data. A high-gain airMAX 2.4 GHz, 13 dBi omni-directional antenna 
ensures signal stability in non-line-of-sight conditions. System power is provided by a 12V, 100 Ah LiPo 
battery and distributed through custom-engineered PCBs made in Altium Designer. These include a 12V 
distribution board and 5V step-down converter equipped with XT60 connectors for modularity. 
Individual fuses were placed at each terminal to ensure high levels of safety. Additionally, safety is 
maintained by a 120A circuit breaker and an emergency stop button for a system wide hard cut.  
1.1.4.
Software 
Our software system is built on a distributed network of computers utilizing Robot Operating System 2 
(ROS2) as the core architecture for communication. To mirror the modular philosophy of our physical 
rover, the system is partitioned into vehicle software, a custom ground station, and specialized remote 
1 

subsystems. The computing architecture is centered around an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano, which serves as 
the primary processing unit for high-demand tasks like camera processing and vision-based navigation. 
This is supported by a network of Raspberry Pi units dedicated to specific subsystems, including the 
robotic arm, driveline, and science payload. To ensure rapid iteration and reliability, we have fully 
adapted our C++ environment to run within Docker containers. This allows us to integrate external 
libraries and modify our environment without operating system constraints. We utilize a system that 
synchronizes Docker builds across the fleet. Images are built on the Jetson and propagated to the 
Raspberry Pi units to ensure version consistency. Our vehicle software supports both manual and 
autonomous modes. This year, we specifically reworked our hardware interfaces to accommodate the 
new drivetrain hardware model, providing improved control for the updated steering system. The ground 
station provides a fully custom GUI environment designed for a smooth, intuitive, and highly informed 
operating experience, allowing for live monitoring of every rover subsystem.

2.
Integration and Testing 
LUSI follows a bottom-up approach to system integration, where subsystems are developed, verified, and 
validated independently before they are integrated with the other subsystems. This reduces integration 
risk and allows for early identification of incompatibilities and issues. After each subsystem has been 
fully integrated onto the rover, the project is currently in its final testing phase where we are focusing on 
run throughs of competition missions and more complicated subsystem test procedures to identify any 
remaining system problems. Our test plan verifies performance at the component, subsystem, and full 
system levels. Individual hardware and software elements are tested against requirements, then 
integrated subsystems are validated for proper interface and functionality. End-to-end system testing 
confirms complete mission operation, including image capture, onboard processing, and downlink, with 
simulations and fault testing used to reduce risk prior to deployment. 
3.
Project Management 
3.1.
Team Organization 
The LUSI team operates under a subsystem-based organizational structure designed to mirror our rovers 
system architecture. Weekly leadership meetings ensure alignment across subsystems, while full-team 
meetings provide progress updates and integration coordination. This system ensures subsystem 
success, design and deliverable ownership, and efficient integration. Our leadership structure is as 
follows: 
 Club President: Overall program management, sponsor and school relations, subsystem 
integration 
 VP/URC Team Lead: Design guidance, subsystem coordination, new-member training 
 Subteam Leads (DCS, Robotic Arm, Electrical, Science, and Software): Responsible for individual 
subteams, including full design, manufacturing, system integration with rover, and training of 
new members. Subteams operate semi-independently during development but converge during 
integration phases. 
3.2.
Project Management Approach (January-May 2026) 
During the second half of the URC cycle, the team transitions from design and development focused 
work to integration and testing focused activities. The January-May timeline is organized in phases.

3.3.
Team Training  
LUSI places a high value on new member engagement and training, subscribing to the philosophy that 
engineering clubs give students a leg up in skills development and success in industry.  At the start of 
each year, weekly training sessions are held where new and existing members learn SOLIDWORKS, 
Altium, C++ ROS, soldering, and more. During our Thursday work meetings, new students can get 
immediate hands-on experience learning from current members of the club. During Sunday meetings, 
we focus on progress overviews, planning, and subsystem integration, in addition to typical club design 
and fabrication work. We prioritize letting members immediately jump in to help, giving them invaluable 
experience while also progressing the rover faster. As part of the DCS teams new strategy for student 
development, weve begun training  our members on welding and CNC milling as well. 
3.4.
Educational and Public Outreach  
LUSI engages in a variety of outreach activities, both in the Lehigh and Bethlehem area community, as 
well as STEM education activities at local schools for grades K-12. We attend all of Lehighs 
engineering-focused admissions events in order to introduce potential and admitted students to our 
club, mission, and projects. Additionally, through Lehighs STEM Squad outreach team, we have had the 
opportunity to present to grade school aged children on why engineering is important, what you can do 
with it, and what our team does. At a science-fair-like event held for middle school students, we held a 
booth with design your own satellite and rover activities. For the past four years we have taken part in 
the Lehigh Valley Space Fest, where we worked to inspire children to learn about space by allowing them 
to drive our rover, and Boeings Engineering Week, where Boeing engineers and students from nearby 
colleges discussed and learned about our work.

6.
Science Plan (only on page 7) 
The systems integrated into our science module serve three key functions: analyzing potential 
soil sample sites, collecting a soil sample of at least 10 cm depth, and analyzing a collected soil sample to 
determine its suitability for supporting biological life. Built at half the footprint of last year's design, our 
2025 science module delivers greater precision and capability across every stage of the mission. With 4 
female square tubing connectors, it supports quick and easy attachment and detachment to our rover 
chassis. 
For analyzing soil sample sites, we collect images and footage using a wide-angle 180 panoramic 
camera and a close-up high-resolution camera, alongside specialized software to analyze stratigraphic 
profiles and sediment layers. With this footage analysis, we build a hypothesis regarding which site has 
the greatest potential for supporting microbial life. 
Our collection platform is driven by a NEO 550 motor along vertically oriented support poles, 
lowering the auger into position to collect a subsurface sample. A servo-controlled hatch discards surface 
material above 10 cm depth, and the retained soil transfers into our testing chamber, which doubles as 
our sample cache. 
Once an adequate sample is in the testing chamber, we begin analyzing it to determine its 
likelihood of carrying microbial life. A load cell verifies that more than 5g has been collected. A 7-in-1 soil 
probe measures nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, pH, electrical conductivity, temperature, and 
moisture. We then perform two chemical experiments, each using a pre-loaded testing vial into which 
the collected soil is introduced. 
The Biuret test detects peptide bonds by reacting copper(II) ions in an alkaline solution with the 
nitrogen atoms in peptide linkages, producing a visible color shift from blue to violet. This confirms the 
presence of proteins and polypeptides, which are only produced by living or once-living organisms. The 
Benedict's test heats the sample with a copper-based alkaline reagent, causing any reducing sugars 
present to donate electrons to the copper ions, shifting the solution from blue through green, yellow, 
and orange depending on sugar concentration. Reducing sugars persist in soil even after proteins have 
degraded, broadening our detection window for past biological activity. Both color changes are 
quantified using our custom-built spectrophotometer, which measures absorbance at specific 
wavelengths corresponding to the reaction products of each test, interpreted against lab-developed 
calibration curves to generate a data-driven hypothesis about the presence of microbial life in our 
sample. 
To ensure safety, we transport all chemicals in lab-grade sealed containers, integrate safety 
measures within our module to guarantee no chemical spillage, and dispose of all chemicals and treated 
soil at Lehigh's Biolabs.
